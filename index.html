Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in our solar system. Despite its proximity to the Sun, it's not the hottest planet (that title belongs to Venus).


Size: Slightly larger than Earth's Moon.
Temperature: Extreme temperature variations, from scorching hot days to freezing cold nights due to its lack of a significant atmosphere.  
Surface: Heavily cratered, suggesting a long history of asteroid impacts.  
Orbit: The fastest orbit of any planet in our solar system, completing a revolution around the Sun in just 88 Earth days.

Venus

Venus is often referred to as Earth's "twin" due to its similar size, mass, and composition. However, its surface conditions are vastly different, making it one of the most hostile environments in our solar system.


Size and Mass: Similar to Earth.
Temperature: The hottest planet in our solar system, with surface temperatures reaching around 864 degrees Fahrenheit (462 degrees Celsius).
Atmosphere: Extremely thick and dense, composed primarily of carbon dioxide. This greenhouse effect traps heat, causing the high temperatures.

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life. Its unique combination of factors, including liquid water, a suitable atmosphere, and a stable climate, make it a habitable world.


Size and Mass: The largest terrestrial planet in our solar system.
Temperature: A relatively stable average temperature of around 59 degrees Fahrenheit (15 degrees Celsius).
Atmosphere: Composed primarily of nitrogen and oxygen, with trace amounts of other gases.

Mars

Mars, often referred to as the Red Planet due to its reddish appearance caused by iron oxide on its surface, is the fourth planet from the Sun. It has been a popular target for exploration due to its potential for past or present life.


Size and Mass: Smaller and less massive than Earth.
Temperature: Cold, with average surface temperatures around -63 degrees Fahrenheit (-53 degrees Celsius).
Atmosphere: Thin and composed primarily of carbon dioxide, with trace amounts of nitrogen, argon, and oxygen.

Jupiter

Jupiter, the fifth planet from the Sun, is the largest planet in our solar system. It's a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen and helium.


Size: More than twice the mass of all the other planets combined.
Great Red Spot: A giant storm that has been raging for centuries.
Moons: Jupiter has over 80 known moons, including the four largest moons discovered by Galileo Galilei, often referred to as the Galilean moons.

Saturn

Saturn, the sixth planet from the Sun, is the second largest planet in our solar system. It's known for its stunning rings, which are the most visible in our solar system.


Size: The second largest planet in our solar system, with a diameter about nine times that of Earth.
Composition: A gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen and helium.  
Rings: Saturn's most famous feature, its rings are made up of countless icy particles ranging in size from dust grains to boulders.

Uranus

Uranus, the seventh planet from the Sun, is an ice giant planet primarily composed of "ices" like water, methane, and ammonia. It's known for its unusual axial tilt, which causes its seasons to be extremely long.


Size: The third largest planet in our solar system.
Composition: Primarily composed of "ices" like water, methane, and ammonia.
Axial Tilt: Uranus is tilted on its side, with its axis almost parallel to the plane of its orbit. This results in extreme seasonal changes.

Neptune

Neptune, the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun, is an ice giant similar to Uranus. It's known for its intense storms and its deep blue color.


Size: The fourth largest planet in our solar system.
Composition: Primarily composed of "ices" like water, methane, and ammonia.
Color: Neptune's deep blue color is due to the absorption of red light by methane in its atmosphere.
